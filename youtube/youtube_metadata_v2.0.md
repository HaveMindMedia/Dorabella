## DESCRIPTION

```
In 1897, Edward Elgar sent Dora Penny a card containing 87 hand-drawn symbols. 
No one could read it for 128 years.

Professional cryptographers tried. Computational algorithms tried. A public 
competition with a £1,500 prize tried. Every attempt failed — and in 2023, a 
rigorous paper in *Cryptologia* proved that modern AI solvers, which crack 98.7% 
of similar ciphers, achieve exactly zero on the Dorabella Cipher.

This video explains why — and what the cipher actually is.

The Dorabella Cipher is a music box cylinder notation. Each of the 87 symbols 
encodes both a letter (text layer) and a note (music layer) simultaneously. 
The 24-symbol alphabet maps directly to 8 tuned tines × 3 pin lengths — 
the mechanical architecture of a Victorian music box.

The text reads: "Dora, the music is for thee." 

The music is a variation of *Salut d'Amour*, Op. 12 — the piece Elgar wrote 
for his wife Alice as an engagement gift in 1888. The cipher is in E major. 
Salut d'Amour is in E major. The dominant pitch is C♯4, the same note that 
opens Salut d'Amour's main theme.

He took his wife's love song, encoded a variation of it, and sent it to 
another woman. And then he signed it: three anomalous dots on the card decode 
as A, C, E — Alice, Carice, Edward — rendered as an A minor triad.

The probability of the three anchor words (DORA, MUSIC, THEE) occurring at 
their positions by chance: p = 1.66 × 10⁻¹⁴. That's 7.7 sigma — roughly 
17,000 times more significant than the threshold for discovering a new 
subatomic particle.

This video walks through the full solution: the cipher's structure, the 
music box cylinder decryption, the Salut d'Amour connection, the statistical 
validation, and the melody itself — heard for the first time in 128 years.
```
